⚔️ Warrington at War

Honouring those who served, sacrificed & survived

Warrington 1914–1918

Warrington made a profound contribution to the First World War. Its wire and wire-rope works — particularly Rylands Brothers — produced vast quantities of barbed wire that stretched across the Western Front. The town's chemical and rubber industries turned to munitions and equipment manufacture. Thousands of local men enlisted in the Warrington Pals battalion and other regiments, many never to return.

The Warrington Cenotaph in Palmyra Square records the sacrifice of over 1,400 men and women. Winwick Hospital served as a military auxiliary hospital. The town's railways became vital arteries for troops and supplies heading south to embarkation ports.

Warrington 1939–1945

Warrington was at the heart of Britain's war effort. RAF Burtonwood became one of the largest US Air Force bases in Europe — some 18,000 American servicemen were stationed here, with the base overhauling and supplying aircraft across the theatre of war. It was a town-within-a-town and transformed local life.

The Risley Royal Ordnance Factory, built rapidly from 1939, employed tens of thousands — mostly women — producing shells, cartridges and explosives. Crosfields chemical works contributed to war production. Bank Quay railway station was a vital supply hub. Warrington suffered bombing raids, and its people endured rationing, evacuees, and the constant anxiety of war.
